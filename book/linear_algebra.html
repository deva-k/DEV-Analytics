Linear Algebra#

Vectors#

Show code cell source Hide code cell source 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A vector is a one-dimensional matrix.

(1)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

The size of the vectors is determined by its rows or how many numbers it has. For example, \(\mathbf{x}\) is \(3\times 1\) meaning it has 3 rows and 1 column.

Vectors that are \(2\times 1\) are said to belong to \(\mathbb{R}^2\), meaning that they belong to the set of real numbers that come paired two at a time. The capital \(\mathbb{R}\) is used to emphasize that you’re looking at real numbers. You also say that \(2\times 1\) vectors, or vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), are a part of two-space.

Vectors in 2 space are represented on the coordiante \((x,y)\) plane by rays. In standard position, the ray representing a vector has its endpoint at the origin and its terminal point (or arrow) at the \((x,y)\) coordinates designated by the column vector. The \(x\) coordinate is in the first row of the vector, and the \(y\) coordinate is in the second row.

(2)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

Here \(\mathbf{u}\) \(\in\) \(\mathbb{R}^2\), and can be represented in the \((x,y)\) as \((1,2)\).

Notice that the vectors have the same lengths and point in the same direction with the same slant. What we did above, is simply moving moving \(x\) or \(y\) steps away from the endpoint. In other words, we simply add the \(\mathbf{u}\) with the \(endpoint\) vector to get a resulting vector

(4)#\[\begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

which is the terminal point \((0,4)\). We shall look at computations on vectors in detail.

Vectors can actually have many number of rows, although its difficult to illustrate vectors that have more than 3 rows, which implies 3 spaces.

For example,

(5)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

where \(\mathbf{w}\) \(\in\) \(\mathbb{R}^3\), and can be represented in the \((x,y,z)\) as \((2,3,4)\). Three-space vectors are represented by three-dimensional figures and arrows pointing to positions in space.

The different geometric transformations performed on vectors include rotations, reflections, expansions, and contractions.

Scalar multiplication#

When you multiply a \(2\times 1\) vector by a scalar, your result is another \(2\times 1\) vector. A scalar is a real number — a constant value. Multiplying a vector by a scalar means that you multiply each element in the vector by the same constant value.

(6)#\[\begin{align} k \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} kv_1 \\ kv_2 \\ \vdots \\ kv_n\end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

where we multiply the vector \(\mathbf{v}\) by \(scalar\) \(k\). Below is an example

(7)#\[\begin{align} 2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 6 \\ -2\end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

Vectors have operations that cause dilations (expansions) and contractions (shrinkages) of the original vector. Both operations of dilation and contraction are accomplished by multiplying the elements in the vector by a scalar.

If the scalar, k, that is multiplying a vector is greater than 1, then the result is a dilation of the original vector. If the scalar, k, is a number between 0 and 1, then the result is a contraction of the original vector.

For example, consider a vector \(\mathbb{a}\)

(8)#\[\begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

multiplying it with a scalar say 3 will result in dilation or expansion and with \(\frac{1}{2}\) results in contraction or shrinking.

(9)#\[\begin{align} 3\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix}, \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

As you can see, the length of the vector is affected by the scalar multiplication, but the direction or angle with the axis (slant) is not changed.

When multiplying a vector by a negative scalar, these things happen:

  1. The size of the vector changes i.e. each element in the vector changes and has a greater absolute value (except with –1).

  2. The direction of the vector reverses.

Multiplying vector \(\mathbb{a}\) with -2 will give

Vector addition and subtraction#

The vectors have to be of the same size to add one another or subtract from one another. This process involves adding or subtracting the corresponding elements in the vectors like a one-to-one match up.

For example

(11)#\[\begin{align} \mathbb{u} + \mathbb{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_n \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \\ u_2 + v_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_n + v_n \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

We can illustrate what is happening when we add 2 vectors. For example

(12)#\[\begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

Vector magnitude#

The magnitude of a vector is also referred to as its length or norm. It is the length of the ray that you see in two-space or three-space. Vectors with more than three rows also have magnitude, and the computation is the same although its not possible to visually picture it.

The magnitude of \(\mathbb{v}\) is denoted by \(\lVert \mathbb{v} \rVert\). The formula is

(14)#\[\begin{align} \lVert \mathbb{v} \rVert = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2 + v_4^2 \dots v_n^2} \end{align}\]

where \(v_1^2, v_2^2, v_3^2, \dots ,v_n^2\) are the elements of the vector.

When you multiplying a vector by a scalar, then the magnitude is also multiplied, i.e. \(\lvert k \rvert \cdot \lVert \mathbb{v} \rVert\). For example

(15)#\[\begin{align} \mathbb{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

then the magnitude is

(16)#\[\begin{align} \lVert \mathbb{v} \rVert = \sqrt{2^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{20} \end{align}\]

By multiplying the vector with 3 results in

(17)#\[\begin{align} 3 \mathbb{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]
(18)#\[\begin{align} \lVert \mathbb{v} \rVert = \sqrt{6^2 + 12^2} = 3\sqrt{20} \end{align}\]

which is simply 3 times the actual magnitude. Although in the case of negative scalar consider the absolute value.

Triangle inequality#

The magnitude of the sum of vectors is always less than or equal to the sum of the magnitudes of the vectors. \(\lVert \mathbb{u} + \mathbb{v}\rVert \le \lVert \mathbb{u}\rVert + \lVert \mathbb{v}\rVert\)
